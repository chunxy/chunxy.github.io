Lagrange Multiplier

Lagrange Multiplier

Standard form of the optimization problem is \[ \begin{aligned} &\min f_0(x) \\ s.t.\quad &f_i(x) \le 0, i = 1,\dots,r \\ &h_i(x) = 0, i = 1,\dots,s \\ \end{aligned} \] Denote the feasible set of \(x\) that satisfies all the requirements in original problem as \(\mathcal X\).

The Lagrangian function is \(L:\R^N \times \R^r \times \R^s \mapsto \R\) with \(dom(L) = \mathcal X \times \R^r \times \R^s\): \[ L(x, \lambda, \mu) = f_0(x) + \sum_{i=1}^r\lambda_if_i(x) + \sum_{i=1}^s\mu_ih_i(x) \] Define the function \(P: \mathcal X \mapsto \R\) as \[ P(x) = \sup\limits_{\lambda,\mu;\lambda_i \ge 0}L(x,\lambda,\mu) \] The primal problem is \[ \min_{x \in \mathcal X} P(x) \] \(P(x) =f_0(x)\) on \(\mathcal X\). Thus the primal problem is equivalent to the original problem. Denote its optimal value as \(p^\star\).

Define the Lagrangian dual function \(D: \R^r \times \R^s \mapsto \R\) as \[ D(\lambda, \mu) = \inf_{x \in \mathcal X}L(x, \lambda, \mu) \] The Lagrangian dual problem is \[ \max_{\lambda,\mu;\lambda_i \ge 0} D(\lambda, \mu) \\ \] Denote its optimal value as \(d^\star\). We always have \(p^\star \ge d^\star\).

  • Proof

    For any feasible \(x \in \mathcal X, (\lambda, \mu) \in dom(D)\), \[ P(x) = \sup\limits_{\lambda,\mu;\lambda_i \ge 0}L(x,\lambda,\mu) \ge L(x,\lambda,\mu) \ge \inf_{x \in \mathcal X}L(x, \lambda, \mu) =D (\lambda, \mu) \] Therefore \(p^\star \ge d^\star\).

\(p^\star \ge d^\star\) is condition called weak duality because it always holds. \(p^\star = d^\star\) is called a strong duality because it does not hold in general. But it does hold in general in [[Convex Optimization|convex optimization]] where \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) are convex and \(h_1,\dots,h_s\) are affine transformations of \(x\), i.e. in the form of \(Ax + b\).

Strong Duality

Assume a strong duality holds, \(x^\star\) is the primal optima, \((\lambda^\star, \mu^\star)\) is the dual optima, then \[ f_0(x^\star) = P(x^\star) = D(\lambda^\star, \mu^\star) \\ \] \[ \begin{gather} f_0(x^\star) = D(\lambda^\star, \mu^\star) = \inf_{x \in \mathcal X}L(x,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \le L(x^\star,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \\ f_0(x^\star) = P(x) = \sup\limits_{\lambda,\mu;\lambda_i \ge 0}L(x^\star,\lambda,\mu) \ge L(x^\star,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \\ L(x^\star,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \le f_0(x^\star) \le L(x^\star,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \end{gather} \]

Therefore, \[ f_0(x^\star) = P(x^\star) = D(\lambda^\star, \mu^\star) = L(x^\star,\lambda^\star,\mu^\star) \]

Karush-Kuhn-Tucker Conditions

In standard optimization problem, KKT conditions refer to the below four:

  • Primal Constraints: \(f_1(x) \dots,f_r(x) \le 0, h_1(x),\dots,h_s(x) = 0\)

  • Dual Constraints: \(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_r \ge 0\)

  • Complementary Constraints: \(\lambda_1f_1(x),\dots,\lambda_rf_r(x) = 0\)

  • Gradient of Lagrangian w.r.t \(x\) vanishes: \[ \nabla f_0(x) + \sum_{i=1}^r\lambda_i\nabla f_i(x) + \sum_{i=1}^s\mu_i\nabla h_i(x) = 0 \]

If the strong duality holds and \((x,\lambda,\mu)\) is the optima, they must satisfy the KKT conditions.

Note that KKT conditions do not imply the existence of a strong duality or optima point.
